# Day 3 - Topic 10: Short Sale Process

## Overview
A short sale occurs when a seller owes more on their property than it's worth and needs the bank to accept less than the full payoff amount. Understanding when and how to use short sales can help sellers avoid foreclosure and allow you to serve clients in difficult situations.

---

## What is a Short Sale?

### Definition

**Short sale:**
> Seller owes more than the property is worth, and the bank agrees to accept less than the total amount owed to avoid foreclosure.

**Example scenario:**
- Property current value: $550,000
- Seller owes: $600,000 (including penalties, interest, liens)
- Bank agrees to accept: $550,000 (takes a loss of $50,000)
- Seller avoids foreclosure

### How It Differs from Regular Sale

**Regular sale:**
- Seller has equity (owes less than property worth)
- Seller pays off mortgage at closing
- Seller receives net proceeds
- Straightforward process

**Short sale:**
- Seller "underwater" (owes more than property worth)
- Bank must approve accepting less than owed
- Seller usually receives little to no proceeds
- Bank negotiation required
- Lengthy process (typically 6+ months)

---

## When to Use a Short Sale

### Seller Situations Requiring Short Sale

**1. Facing foreclosure**
- Can't make mortgage payments
- Fallen behind on payments
- Bank threatening foreclosure
- Needs to avoid foreclosure on credit report

**2. Financial hardship**
- Job loss
- Medical emergency
- Divorce
- Business failure
- Cannot afford property anymore

**3. Underwater on mortgage**
- Property value dropped since purchase
- Refinanced and took cash out
- Liens accumulated (contractor, tax, judgment)
- Total debt exceeds current value

**4. Can't sell at market price**
- Regular sale wouldn't pay off mortgage
- Would need to bring money to closing
- Seller doesn't have cash to pay difference
- Short sale is only option besides foreclosure

---

## The Numbers Behind Short Sales

### Example Scenario

**Property details:**
- Current market value: $550,000
- Original mortgage: $500,000
- Seller stopped paying 2 years ago
- Accumulated interest and penalties: $75,000
- Contractor lien: $15,000
- Property tax lien: $10,000

**Total owed:**
- First mortgage: $500,000
- Penalties and interest: $75,000
- Contractor lien: $15,000
- Property tax lien: $10,000
- **Total: $600,000**

**The problem:**
- Property worth: $550,000
- Seller owes: $600,000
- Seller is $50,000 underwater
- Seller has no cash to make up difference

**Short sale solution:**
- Sell for $550,000
- Bank accepts $550,000 (instead of $600,000)
- Bank writes off $50,000 loss
- Seller avoids foreclosure
- Everyone gets paid (agents, attorney, transfer taxes)
- Deal closes

## How Short Sales Work

### The Process

**1. Seller contacts you**
- Seller in financial distress
- Can't afford mortgage payments
- Owes more than property worth
- Facing foreclosure or already in foreclosure process

**2. Determine if short sale is appropriate**
- Run preliminary title search (Regency Title can help)
- Calculate total amount owed
- Determine current market value
- Assess if seller qualifies (hardship required)

**3. List property**
- Price at current market value (not amount owed)
- Disclose it's a short sale in listing
- Market normally to attract buyers
- Warn buyers: lengthy process, bank must approve

**4. Receive offer**
- Buyer makes offer
- You submit to seller
- Seller accepts (contingent on bank approval)

**5. Submit to bank for approval**
- Seller's attorney coordinates
- Submit offer, comps, hardship letter
- Bank reviews
- Bank may counter, accept, or reject

**6. Negotiate with bank**
- Bank may want more money
- You provide comps showing market value
- Explain property condition
- Justify offer price
- Bank weighs: accept short sale vs. go through foreclosure

**7. Bank approves**
- Issues approval letter
- Sets closing deadline (usually short - 30 days)
- Buyer must be ready to close quickly

**8. Close transaction**
- Standard closing process
- Bank receives proceeds
- Liens paid off
- Seller relieved of debt (usually)
- Seller may owe taxes on forgiven debt (consult CPA)

## Bank's Perspective

### Why Banks Accept Short Sales

**Foreclosure is expensive for banks:**
- Legal fees (attorneys, court costs)
- Time-consuming (can take 1-2 years)
- Property maintenance costs
- Vandalism risk
- Market risk (property value may drop further)
- Property taxes pile up
- Property condition may deteriorate

**Short sale is faster and cheaper:**
- Avoid legal process
- Get property sold quickly
- Cut losses sooner
- Less expensive overall
- Seller cooperates (vs. fighting foreclosure)

**Instructor's explanation:**
> "Foreclosure is expensive, legally complex, and time-consuming for banks. They'd rather accept short sale, get some money back, and move on."

---

## The Short Sale Timeline

### Realistic Expectations

**Typical timeline: 6-12 months**

**Month 1-2: Preparation**
- Seller engages attorney
- Financial hardship package prepared
- Property listed
- Marketing begins

**Month 2-3: Receive offer**
- Buyer makes offer
- Seller accepts (subject to bank approval)
- Submit to bank

**Month 3-6: Bank review**
- Bank receives package
- Assigns to loss mitigation department
- Reviews financials
- Orders BPO (Broker Price Opinion) or appraisal
- Makes decision

**Month 6-8: Negotiation**
- Bank may counter
- You justify price with comps
- Back and forth negotiation
- Eventually reach agreement

**Month 8+: Approval and closing**
- Bank issues approval
- 30-day closing deadline (typical)
- Scramble to close quickly
- Deal closes

**Warning to buyers:**
> "Short sales take 6+ months typically. If you need to move quickly, this may not be right for you."

## Calling the Seller's Bank

### Your Role

**What you can do:**
- Call bank on seller's behalf (with authorization)
- Explain situation (job loss, illness, etc.)
- Request penalty reduction
- Negotiate lower payoff
- Provide market analysis

**What you can't do:**
- Access seller's account without authorization
- Make promises about seller's finances
- Negotiate without seller and attorney approval

### The Hardship Conversation

**Script:**
> "I'm calling on behalf of [Seller Name] regarding their mortgage account ending in [last 4 digits]. [Seller] has experienced a financial hardship due to [job loss/medical emergency/divorce] and can no longer afford the mortgage payments. The property is worth approximately $550,000, but they owe $600,000. We'd like to discuss a short sale to avoid foreclosure. Is there someone in your loss mitigation department I can speak with?"

**What bank wants to know:**
- Why can't seller pay? (hardship reason)
- Has seller tried to make payments?
- What is property worth?
- How much will bank get in short sale vs. foreclosure?
- Is this legitimate hardship or strategic default?

**What helps your case:**
- Legitimate hardship (not just "don't want to pay anymore")
- Seller cooperating fully
- Buyer already lined up
- Market analysis showing value
- Quick closing possible

## Attorney's Role in Short Sale

### Why Attorney Handles Most Negotiations

**Attorney responsibilities:**
- Prepare hardship package
- Submit financial documents to bank
- Negotiate with bank's attorney
- Navigate legal complexities
- Protect seller's interests
- Coordinate closing

**Your role:**
- Find buyer
- Market property
- Provide comps and market data
- Support attorney's negotiations
- Manage buyer expectations
- Keep deal together during long process

**Team approach:**
- You + seller's attorney + buyer's agent + buyer's attorney + bank's loss mitigation + bank's attorney = complex transaction

## What Seller Gets from Short Sale

### Financial Outcome

**Typical result:**
- Seller gets SOME money (rare)
- More common: Seller gets $0
- At minimum: Pays Realtors, attorneys, transfer taxes

**Why seller does it anyway:**
- Avoids foreclosure on credit report
- Foreclosure = 7 years on credit, very damaging
- Short sale = less damaging (still impacts credit but better than foreclosure)
- Relieves debt burden
- Can move on with life

**Potential tax consequence:**
- Forgiven debt may be taxable income
- Example: Bank forgives $50,000, IRS may treat as income
- Seller should consult CPA or tax attorney
- Some exceptions for primary residence

### Emotional Considerations

**Seller feelings:**
- Embarrassed
- Stressed
- Feeling like failure
- Desperate
- Relieved when process starts

**Your role:**
- Be compassionate
- Non-judgmental
- Explain process clearly
- Set realistic expectations
- Support them through difficult time

## Listing a Short Sale Property

### Disclosure Requirements

**MLS listing:**
- Mark as "Short Sale"
- Note "Bank approval required"
- Set realistic expectations in description

**Example description:**
> "Short Sale - Bank Approval Required. Beautiful 4BR colonial in great neighborhood. Seller has accepted offer pending bank approval. Process typically takes 6+ months. Seller will consider backup offers."

### Pricing Strategy

**Price at current market value:**
- NOT at what seller owes
- Use comps like any other listing
- Bank wants to see property priced correctly
- Overpricing delays sale
- Underpricing raises red flags with bank

**Example:**
- Seller owes: $600,000
- Market value: $550,000
- **List at: $549,900** (market value, not amount owed)

## Working with Buyers on Short Sales

### Setting Expectations

**What to tell buyers:**
> "This is a short sale, which means the bank must approve our offer even if the seller accepts. This process typically takes 6-12 months. You'll need to be patient and flexible. The seller cannot make any repairs, and the property is sold as-is. If you need to move quickly or want a property in perfect condition, this may not be the right fit."

**Buyer must understand:**
- Long timeline (6+ months typical)
- No guarantee of bank approval
- Property sold as-is
- No seller repairs
- Must remain qualified for entire time
- Backup offers may be submitted
- Bank may counter their offer

### Finding the Right Buyer

**Ideal short sale buyer:**
- Not in a rush
- Flexible timeline
- Likes the property enough to wait
- Pre-approved and will stay approved
- Understands process
- Patient personality

**Wrong buyer:**
- Needs to move in 30 days
- Lease ending soon
- School year starting soon
- Low patience
- Wants perfect property

## Common Short Sale Challenges

### Challenge #1: Second Mortgages/HELOCs

**Problem:**
- First mortgage: $500K
- Second mortgage/HELOC: $100K
- Total: $600K
- Property worth: $550K

**Both banks must agree:**
- First mortgage bank gets priority
- Second mortgage bank gets little or nothing
- Second mortgage bank may refuse (they get almost nothing)
- Requires negotiation with both banks

**Solution:**
- First mortgage bank may pay second bank small amount ($1,000-$5,000) to release lien
- Negotiate settlement with second mortgage bank
- May require seller to pay something (payment plan)

### Challenge #2: Buyer Loses Patience

**Problem:**
- Month 4: Buyer still waiting
- Found another property they like
- Tired of waiting
- Wants to walk away

**Solution:**
- Regular updates to buyer (even if no news)
- Manage expectations from beginning
- Have backup offers ready
- Consider releasing buyer, starting over with new buyer

### Challenge #3: Bank Delays

**Problem:**
- Bank takes 4 months to respond
- Loses paperwork
- Requests same documents multiple times
- Different person each time you call

**Solution:**
- Document everything
- Resubmit documents immediately when requested
- Escalate to supervisor if excessive delays
- Seller's attorney may need to involve their attorney
- Patience and persistence

### Challenge #4: Property Condition Deteriorates

**Problem:**
- Seller moved out months ago
- Property vacant for 6 months during bank approval
- Vandalism, weather damage, neglect
- Buyer wants to renegotiate based on new condition

**Solution:**
- Seller should maintain property during process
- Regular inspections
- Address issues immediately
- May need to renegotiate with buyer and bank
