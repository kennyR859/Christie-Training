## üèõÔ∏è Post-2008 Appraisal System

### Why Appraisers Can't Be Chosen

**2008 Crisis Problem:**
- Lenders hired appraisers directly
- Pressure to inflate values
- Appraisers complied to keep business
- Result: Overvalued properties, market crash

**Current System:**
- All appraisers belong to AMCs (Appraisal Management Companies)
- Lenders order from AMC, AMC assigns random appraiser
- Prevents direct lender-appraiser relationships
- No pressure to hit specific values

### Unity's Local Advantage

**Unity Uses Local Appraisers:**
- NJ and PA appraisers who know the market
- Understand neighborhood nuances
- Familiar with school district premiums
- Know local market trends

**Big Banks Use National AMCs:**
- Out-of-state appraisers
- Don't understand local markets
- Miss important comparable sales
- More likely to under-appraise

## üîß Solution 1: Negotiate with Appraiser

### Pat's Direct Approach

**What Pat Does:**
- Calls appraiser after report received
- Reviews comparable sales used
- Points out recent sales appraiser missed
- Discusses adjustments made
- Questions methodology if questionable

**Success Rate:**
- Can often get $10,000-$15,000 increase
- Especially if appraiser missed recent comps
- Or didn't properly adjust for features

**Example:**
```
Appraisal: $490,000
Contract: $510,000
Gap: $20,000

Pat's Call:
- Points out 3 recent sales at $505K-$515K
- Discusses property upgrades not fully credited
- Reviews adjustment calculations

Result: Revised appraisal $502,000
New Gap: Only $8,000 (manageable)
```

## üîß Solution 2: Order Second Appraisal

### Unity's Unique Offer

**Pat's Promise:**
> "If we do another appraisal and it comes in higher, I'll pay for it. If it comes in same or lower, customer pays for second one too, but we'll use the higher of the two."

**Process:**
1. Pat orders second appraisal from different AMC
2. Different appraiser assigned
3. Second opinion obtained

**Outcomes:**
```
Best Case: Second appraisal comes in higher
- Unity uses higher appraisal
- Unity PAYS for second appraisal
- Customer only paid for first one
- Deal saved

Worst Case: Second appraisal same or lower
- Unity uses HIGHER of the two
- Customer pays for both appraisals
- Still better than deal falling apart
```

## üîß Solution 3: Adjust Down Payment Percentage

### Creative Financing Solution

**Scenario:**
```
Purchase Price: $600,000
Appraisal: $570,000
Original Plan: 25% down ($150,000)
Gap: $30,000 problem
```

**Solution:**
```
Keep Same Cash Amount:
- Still put down $150,000 cash
- But on $570,000 appraisal
- New percentage: 26.3% down
- Loan: $420,000 (instead of $450,000)
- Still NO PMI (over 25%)
- Customer uses same money, just different ratio
```

**When This Works:**
- Customer has the cash
- Just changes the math/ratios
- Qualifies income-wise for new payment
- Still avoids PMI if stays above 20%

**If Still Short:**
```
More Aggressive Approach:
- Appraised: $550,000
- Customer has: $150,000 saved
- Put down 10% ($55,000) instead of 25%
- Loan: $495,000
- Now has PMI (~$250/month)
- But keeps $95,000 in bank
- Gets the house at negotiated price

Trade-off:
- Pays PMI for 2 years
- But achieves homeownership
- Can remove PMI after appreciation
```

## üîß Solution 4: Renegotiate Price

### Negotiation Strategies

**Approach 1: Meet in Middle**
```
Contract: $510,000
Appraisal: $490,000
Gap: $20,000

Proposal: Split difference
New Price: $500,000
Buyer pays extra: $10,000
Seller reduces: $10,000
```

**Approach 2: Seller Reduces to Appraisal**
```
Contract: $510,000
Appraisal: $490,000

Present appraisal to seller:
"Independent third-party appraiser valued at $490K.
Buyer's lender won't lend more than appraised value.
Request price reduction to $490,000."
```

**Approach 3: Seller Concession Trade**
```
Contract: $510,000
Appraisal: $490,000

Proposal:
"Maintain $510,000 price,
BUT seller provides $20,000 seller concession
toward buyer's closing costs and down payment gap"
```

## üìä Pat's Talking Points to Customers

### When Under-Appraisal Occurs

**Positive Framing:**
> "Look, you put 10% down now on paper. The market's been going up 6% every year. In the next 2-3 years, if the rate stays the same, you can always take out the PMI and it'll be the same thing, and you'll have a lower payment. Plus you'll have been living in YOUR home, building equity, rather than paying rent."

**The Math:**
```
Scenario: Adjust from 25% down to 10% down due to appraisal

Year 0: Buy at $550K, 10% down, paying PMI
Payment: $2,900 + $250 PMI = $3,150/month

Year 2: Property appreciates to $618K (6% annual)
Equity: $55K (down) + $68K (appreciation) + $10K (principal) = $133K
Equity %: 21.5%
Action: Remove PMI
New Payment: $2,900/month (no PMI)

Benefit: Still got the house, lived there 2 years, PMI only cost $6,000 total
Alternative: Waited 2 years, house now $618K, need $150K down instead of $55K
```

## üö´ Preventing Appraisal Issues

### Agent's Role Before Appraisal

**1. Research Comparable Sales**
- Pull 3-5 best comps in MLS
- Recent sales (last 3-6 months)
- Similar size, condition, features
- Same school district
- Send to Pat before appraisal

**2. Prepare Property**
- Ensure property shows well for appraiser
- Clean, decluttered
- All systems functional and demonstrated
- Recent upgrades highlighted

**3. Provide Comp Packet**
- If listing agent: Offer to meet appraiser
- Bring professional comp packet
- Include recent sales data
- Don't be pushy, just informative

**4. Set Realistic Expectations**
- Don't encourage buyers to overpay
- Hot market doesn't mean ignoring comps
- Appraisal based on recent CLOSED sales
- Pending sales don't count
